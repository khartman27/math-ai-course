# Draft Introduction: Hybrid Reduced-Order Modeling for Nonlinear and Data-Driven Systems

Reduced-order modeling has become a central tool for making large-scale simulations computationally tractable without sacrificing essential dynamical behavior. In many applications, including nonlinear partial differential equations, inverse problems, and data-driven inference, the cost of repeatedly evaluating high-dimensional models is prohibitive. Reduced-order models (ROMs) address this challenge by representing the state space with a small number of dominant modes and by approximating expensive nonlinear terms through efficient interpolation or projection strategies. Among these methods, the Discrete Empirical Interpolation Method (DEIM) has emerged as a particularly influential framework because it offers a practical way to reduce the cost of nonlinear evaluations while retaining accuracy.

The success of DEIM has motivated several important extensions. Bonomi et al. (2016) introduced a matrix-based variant designed to accelerate the treatment of nonlinear Jacobians in parametrized PDEs, demonstrating that algebraic structure can be exploited to improve efficiency in Newton-type solvers. Saibaba (2020) developed a randomized DEIM approach that reduces the computational burden of basis and index selection, making the method more scalable for large problems. Peherstorfer et al. (2013) proposed a localized formulation that uses regional subspaces to better capture spatially varying behavior, while preserving the efficiency benefits of reduced bases. More recently, Bukac et al. (2024) explored deep-learning-based reduced-order modeling for parametrized and data-driven domains, showing that ROMs can be adapted to settings where geometric structure is complex or partially unavailable. Hendryx Lyons (2024) further broadened the scope of DEIM by showing that its interpolatory mechanism can be useful in data summarization and class identification, extending the method beyond traditional PDE-based applications.

Together, these contributions suggest that the field of reduced-order modeling is moving from purely physics-driven approximation toward more flexible, hybrid frameworks that combine numerical interpolation, machine learning, and data-adaptive structure. This trend is especially promising for problems characterized by nonlinearities, changing domains, and large datasets. Nevertheless, several important challenges remain unresolved. First, the theoretical connection between DEIM approximation errors and performance in nontraditional tasks such as classification and clustering is still underdeveloped. In other words, it is not yet clear how guarantees for approximation quality translate into reliable downstream performance in data-science settings. Second, the robustness and scalability of these methods remain limited when confronted with noise, outliers, complex geometries, and very large-scale data. These issues are particularly acute for methods that rely on expensive basis construction, repeated interpolation, or high-dimensional geometric representations.

These open problems motivate a new research direction that seeks to integrate the strengths of existing approaches into a unified framework. A promising path is to combine localization, randomization, and learning-informed structure selection so that reduced-order methods remain accurate while also being computationally efficient and resilient to complex data regimes. Such a framework could provide a bridge between classical ROM theory and modern data-driven modeling, opening the door to more robust approximations in nonlinear systems and high-dimensional scientific computing.

In this paper, we propose a hybrid DEIM-based reduced-order modeling framework that couples localized interpolation, randomized basis construction, and learning-guided adaptation to improve robustness and scalability for nonlinear and data-driven systems.
