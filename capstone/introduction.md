# Introduction

Reduced-order modeling has become a central component of modern scientific computing because many high-fidelity simulations are too expensive to run repeatedly in parameter studies, optimization, control, uncertainty quantification, or real-time prediction. In systems governed by nonlinear partial differential equations, the full state dimension is often very large, and the cost of evaluating the governing operators can dominate the overall computational workflow. Reduced-order models (ROMs) address this difficulty by representing the solution in a low-dimensional subspace and by approximating expensive nonlinear terms with cheaper surrogates. This perspective is especially relevant in applications where accuracy must be preserved while computational cost is substantially reduced (Bonomi et al., 2016; Peherstorfer et al., 2013).

A persistent challenge in reduced-order modeling is to maintain accuracy while controlling the cost of the reduced approximation. Classical ROMs often perform well when the solution manifold is well represented by a small number of dominant modes, but they can become inaccurate when the dynamics are strongly nonlinear, highly localized, or sensitive to parameter variation. In such settings, the cost of evaluating nonlinear terms and Jacobians may remain prohibitive unless the approximation strategy is designed to exploit structure. The Discrete Empirical Interpolation Method (DEIM) was introduced to address this bottleneck by approximating nonlinear terms through a sparse set of interpolation indices and a reduced basis. Its subsequent extensions, including matrix-based, randomized, and localized formulations, have improved both the efficiency and flexibility of reduced-order methods (Saibaba, 2020; Peherstorfer et al., 2013; Bonomi et al., 2016).

Despite these advances, several important difficulties remain unresolved. First, many ROMs are still fragile when applied to problems with complex geometry, sharp local features, or highly nonuniform behavior. These challenges are particularly relevant in settings where the solution exhibits strong spatial variation and where a single global basis is insufficient to describe the full dynamics. Localized and data-adaptive strategies offer a promising response, but they often require additional modeling choices that are not yet fully standardized (Peherstorfer et al., 2013; Bukac et al., 2024). Second, the theoretical guarantees available for DEIM and related methods are most developed in classical approximation settings and are less well understood in emerging data-driven and hybrid contexts. As reduced-order methods increasingly incorporate learning-based structure, clustering, or domain-aware representations, the connection between approximation error and downstream performance becomes more difficult to characterize (Hendryx Lyons, 2024; Bukac et al., 2024).

These limitations motivate two open problems in the field. The first is the need for reduced-order methods that remain accurate and robust in the presence of strong nonlinearity, complex geometry, and localized dynamics without requiring excessively large reduced spaces. The second is the need for principled strategies that combine efficiency, adaptivity, and mathematical rigor in hybrid ROMs, particularly when the model must operate across changing parameter regimes or under limited prior structure. These issues are central to the continued development of reduced-order modeling as a reliable tool for large-scale scientific computation (Saibaba, 2020; Bonomi et al., 2016; Bukac et al., 2024).

In this paper, we develop a hybrid reduced-order modeling framework that combines localization, randomized basis construction, and learning-guided adaptation to improve robustness and scalability for nonlinear systems. Our objective is to provide a practical and theoretically informed approach for reducing the cost of high-dimensional simulations while preserving the essential dynamics of complex problems.
