# Gaps in Reduced-Order Modeling Research

Reduced-order modeling has advanced substantially through DEIM-based methods, but several important gaps remain in the literature. First, the theory for these methods is still strongest in classical function-approximation settings and less developed for nontraditional tasks such as classification, clustering, and data summarization. In particular, it remains unclear how standard DEIM error bounds translate into reliable performance when the goal is not merely to approximate a nonlinear operator but also to preserve interpretability and detect structure in data (Hendryx Lyons, 2024). Second, existing approaches often rely on strong assumptions about geometry, sparsity, or smoothness, which limits their robustness in settings with complex spatial features, noisy measurements, or irregular domains. Localized and learning-informed variants improve flexibility, but they still face challenges when the system exhibits sharp transitions, moving interfaces, or highly nonuniform behavior (Peherstorfer et al., 2013; Bukac et al., 2024). Third, scalability remains a major bottleneck. Although randomized methods reduce the cost of basis construction and interpolation-point selection, many ROM procedures still require expensive matrix operations or repeated recomputation of reduced structures as the problem size grows (Saibaba, 2020; Bonomi et al., 2016). 

A further gap concerns the practical selection of reduced-model structure. The literature does not yet provide broadly accepted rules for choosing the number of modes, interpolation points, or local subspaces, nor for deciding when to switch between local models or update the reduced basis during simulation (Peherstorfer et al., 2013; Saibaba, 2020). This is particularly important for nonlinear and time-dependent systems, where the most effective reduced basis may change over time or across parameter regimes. Finally, there is a need for more unified frameworks that combine localization, randomization, and machine learning in a principled way. The current literature shows promising progress in each direction separately, but fewer methods integrate these ideas into a single robust strategy for large-scale nonlinear systems (Bonomi et al., 2016; Bukac et al., 2024).

Open questions and actionable gaps:

- How can DEIM-based error estimates be extended from approximation error to downstream tasks such as classification, clustering, and data summarization?
- How can reduced-order methods be made more robust to noise, outliers, irregular geometries, and sharp local features?
- What principled criteria should govern the choice of basis size, interpolation points, and local subspace updates in nonlinear problems?
- Can localization, randomization, and learning-based adaptation be combined in a single framework that remains mathematically justified and computationally efficient?
- How can these methods be scaled to high-dimensional fluid-dynamics problems without sacrificing accuracy or stability?
